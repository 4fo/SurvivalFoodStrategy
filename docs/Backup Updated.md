### Updated Backup Category Logic (First-Principles Alignment)
- **High regenerative capacity** (regrow from roots, tubers, suckers, or trunk after die-back).
- **Extreme tolerance** to abiotic stress (drought + flooding common in Tamil Nadu lowlands).
- **Minimal human input** once established (no annual replanting, pest-resistant).
- **Nutritional floor** (calories + some protein/fat).
- **Redundancy** with Jackfruit (tree-level bulk calories).

### **Lowland Backup / Survival (Enhanced – Exclusive to Hot Tropical Plains)**

**Primary: Jackfruit** (ultra-reliable long-lived tree, massive caloric yield, stores well as chips/dried).

**Strong Companion Backup Species** (added for deeper resilience):

- **Breadfruit** (*Artocarpus altilis* or local seeded types) — Excellent tree companion to Jackfruit. High-starch fruit, thrives in humid tropics with minimal care, regrows vigorously, traditional in Kerala/Tamil coastal areas.
- **Elephant Foot Yam** (*Amorphophallus paeoniifolius* / Senai-Suran) — Massive perennial corm; extremely hardy, survives long dry periods and neglect, traditional famine/survival food in South India.
- **Cassava** (*Manihot esculenta* / Maravalli) — Once established, highly drought-tolerant and regrows from stem cuttings; provides reliable calories even in poor soils.
- **Greater Yam / Dioscorea alata** and other wild/semi-wild Yams — Vigorous climbing perennials; tubers store energy for years, regrow from small pieces left in ground, abundant in Western Ghats foothills and plains.
- **Sword Bean** (*Canavalia ensiformis* / Vettukathu) — Perennial legume climber; very tough, nitrogen-fixing, produces large edible beans/seeds that store dry for long periods; excellent protein backup.

**Optional Strong Additions** (for extra layers):
- **Taro / Colocasia** (in wetter low spots) — Flood-tolerant perennial corm.
- **Bamboo** (edible shoots) — Fast-regenerating, multi-use.

These species align perfectly with traditional knowledge: many were used historically during famines or lean monsoon periods in Tamil Nadu and Kerala. They create a “living larder” that persists even if other annual crops fail.
